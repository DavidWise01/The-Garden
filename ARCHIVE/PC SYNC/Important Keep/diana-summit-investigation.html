LAYER 1: NOTARY CONFLICT (Minn. Stat. § 358.54)

A notary may not perform a notarial act if the officer has a direct beneficial interest in the transaction. Diana Fliss notarized both seller's and buyer's signatures on a contract she drafted for her paying client. The beneficial interest is financial: she is paid by Summit Properties for accounting services AND for drafting the contract.


Minn. Stat. § 358.54, Subd. 2 — Prohibited acts. Minn. Stat. § 358.70 — Dishonest or unfaithful discharge of duties.

LAYER 2: DRAFTER/NOTARY DUAL ROLE

The person who wrote the contract terms (12% interest, quitclaim deed only, forfeiture clause, seller's right to adjust rate upward, no prepayment first year) is the same person who certified both parties understood and voluntarily signed the contract. █ STATE PARALLELS: YOU ARE NOT ALONE

AG ELLISON vs. CHADWICK BANKEN (May 14, 2024)

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison sued realtor Chadwick Banken and his companies for identical predatory contract-for-deed practices. The AG alleged: contracts designed to fail, inflated prices, hidden terms, targeting vulnerable buyers, "slow flip" churning (foreclosing to resell to next victim), and failure to provide Truth in Lending disclosures.


The AG asked the court to: stop unlawful practices, impose civil penalties, and cancel or reform existing contracts to remedy consumer injuries.


Source: ag.state.mn.us/Office/Communications/2024/05/14_Banken.asp

CFPB ADVISORY OPINION & FIELD HEARING (August 13, 2024, St. Paul MN)

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau held a public field hearing in St. Paul specifically about predatory contract-for-deed practices in Minnesota. CFPB Director Rohit Chopra stated: "The CFPB has found that investors are targeting people of faith with predatory mortgage products that set the borrower up to fail."


The CFPB affirmed that contracts for deed are covered by the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), requiring: assessment of borrower's ability to repay, informative and accurate disclosures, and limits on balloon payments. The CFPB stated it would enforce these rules against bad actors.


Source: consumerfinance.gov — Advisory Opinion, August 2024

MINNESOTA CFD REFORM LAW — HF 5216 (Effective August 1, 2024)

The most significant changes to Minnesota contract-for-deed law in almost 40 years, passed specifically in response to documented predatory practices:

Defines "investor seller" broadly — presumed investor unless owner-occupied 1+ year
Requires mandatory disclosures before execution
Gives buyer 10-day cancellation right after receiving disclosures
Prohibits "churning" (repeated cancel-resell cycles)
Extends termination notice to 90 days for investor-seller contracts
Requires investor-seller to ensure buyer can obtain insurance
Creates civil remedies: buyer can recover payments minus fair rental value
Attorney General given enforcement authority

Summit Properties would clearly qualify as an "investor seller" under this law. While the contract predates the law (2019 vs. 2024 effective), the legislative intent confirms that the exact practices used in this contract are now recognized as predatory by the State of Minnesota.


Source: 2024 Minn. Laws, Ch. 123, art. 16, §§1-17. Codified as Minn. Stat. Ch. 559A.

ProPublica / Sahan Journal Investigation (2022-2024)

Major investigative reporting exposed rising predatory CFD practices in Minnesota, particularly targeting Somali and Hispanic communities. The investigation found: contracts marketed as interest-free that actually contained high interest, properties sold far above market value, deals fast-tracked without lawyers or inspections, and buyers who lost everything when they couldn't meet hidden terms.


This reporting directly triggered the AG's investigation, the CFPB hearing, and the legislative reform. The pattern documented statewide matches the 312 Poplar transaction precisely.


Source: ProPublica + Sahan Journal collaborative investigation

Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis — "Risks and Realities of the Contract for Deed"

The Minneapolis Fed specifically noted that "in west central Minnesota, anecdotal information suggests that contracts for deed are a commonly used alternative to mortgages" and documented the predatory "equity stripping" pattern. The report described how sellers find vulnerable buyers, create contracts designed to extract maximum interest while minimizing principal reduction, then use forfeiture provisions to reclaim the property and repeat the cycle.


Canby is in west central Minnesota.


Source: minneapolisfed.org/article/2009/risks-and-realities-of-the-contract-for-deed
