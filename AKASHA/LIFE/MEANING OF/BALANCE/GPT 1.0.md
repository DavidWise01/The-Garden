## **Section 1 — Purpose**

This document establishes a governing principle for evaluating the stability, fairness, and sustainability of complex systems, including economic, technological, and social infrastructures.

The principle defines the minimum structural requirements necessary to prevent systemic failure caused by oversimplified (binary) decision frameworks.

---

## **Section 2 — Definitions**

### 2.1 Binary System

A system that resolves decisions, control, or value allocation exclusively between two opposing states (e.g., profit vs. cost, safety vs. growth, human vs. machine), without a mediating mechanism.

---

### 2.2 Ternary System

A system consisting of three functional components:

* **Opposing Forces (±1):** Competing or balancing variables (e.g., production vs. constraint)
* **Mediating Layer (0):** An independent stabilizing mechanism that prevents premature resolution between opposing forces

---

### 2.3 Mediating Layer (“The Gap”)

A structural constraint, oversight mechanism, or governing process that:

* prevents forced binary resolution
* preserves system flexibility
* enables adaptive correction over time

Examples include:

* regulatory oversight
* audit systems
* feedback loops
* human judgment layers

---

## **Section 3 — Core Principle**

A system that operates solely on binary resolution increases the probability of instability, failure, or exploitative outcomes under conditions of complexity.

A system that preserves a third, mediating dimension increases its capacity for:

* stability
* adaptability
* long-term sustainability

---

## **Section 4 — Operational Rule**

For any system involving competing interests or variables:

```text
If only two forces are present → system is structurally unstable under scale or stress.

If a third, independent mediating layer is present → system gains capacity for stabilization and correction.
```
